Deep Dive Coding

This is being updated with Fullstack info.

I started learning Java in January 2020. I was confident that I would at least enjoy coding - it puts a lot of the interests that drive my hobbies to work together - but I was surprised to find how engrossing the process is for me. I picked the basics up very quickly, pushed myself with exercises on CodingBat, and within a few weeks I was helping my classmates when our instructors were busy. I organized weekly study sessions at the CNM library and created a collaborative learning community to support all of us.

Thanks to Deep Dive, I am proficient with IntelliJ IDE and Java SE 8+. I have experience with JUnit4 and 5 testing , relational database design using MySQL queries, RESTful services, Android ViewModel architecture incorporating ReactiveX tasks, and more, which you can examine above. I am also familiar with Scrum and Agile development methods.

COVID-19 gave me a crash course in remote work. I led my team in daily check-ins and planning calls, and managed our workflow by creating an Asana project to organize ideas and assign tasks. We leaned hard into the use of GitHub branches and learned as we worked. Without my instructors in the next room to answer questions, I did a lot of independent research to fix bugs and work out implementations.

Since then, I've continued to refine my Java skills and develop my personal Android project: Choppit is at the stage of minimum viability as an Android app that scrapes HTML and generates simplified recipes with no ads, videos, life stories, etc. My remaining design goals are significantly more complex than what I've accomplished, so I'm mostly content to pause development here.

In April of 2021 I decided to go back for round two at Deep Dive; I'm currently working through a fully remote session of the Fullstack web development program. I've enjoyed adding JavaScript's functional programming style to Java, and working with frontend systems like CSS Grids and React-Bootstrap is an interesting contrast to Android Fragments. I've gotten much more comfortable with the minutiae of tools Slack, Git and Asana.

My next project will be to branch off Choppit mobile and rebuild it as a mini-monolith server app for personal use; a few pages of Bootstrap UI and some JSON should get it to a point where it can be deployed on a desktop computer and accessed locally from any device.

Verizon Wireless

I worked at a Verizon Wireless call center from 2010 to 2018. The majority of my experience is in tech support; the Albuquerque center was among the first to support complex services like international roaming, home/mobile internet devices, and network extenders (micro cell towers that connect to the Verizon network through a wired internet connection) on top of all the changes in cell phone technology over the last decade. I saw each of these as an opportunity to develop expertise with each new set of procedures, challenges and work-arounds as fast as possible.

I got my A+ certification to move up in Tech Support, and my Network+ because it looked cool. I prided myself on the ability to guide even the least confident and most tech-phobic customers through their router admin screens to configure DHCP reservations, port forwarding, occasionally even QoS and DMZ settings - without being able to see their computer screen. Through these experiences, I became highly skilled at applying research, documentation and critical thinking to problems. I also perfected the ability to communicate between laypersons and technical experts.
